Project Overview
What This Project Does

This project implements ideas from the SQLBarber paper to generate SQL workloads whose execution-cost distribution resembles a target (ground-truth) workload. The core idea: normalize each query's execution cost into the range [0, 10 000], bin queries into 10 equal intervals of width 1 000 (Bin 0 = 0–1 000, Bin 9 = 9 000–10 000), and compare the resulting histograms. The Profiling Stage runs a set of SQL templates against the database to understand how much cost each template can generate — forming the foundation for later refinement.

Profiling Analysis
Distribution Insights
🔺
Overrepresented

Bins 0 (0–1 k) and 5 (5–6 k) are heavily overrepresented in the profiling output. Profiling generates 36 queries in Bin 0 vs. 20 in ground truth, and 42 vs. 29 in Bin 5.

🔻
Underrepresented

Bins 3 and 8 are notably underrepresented — profiling contributes only 11 and 5 queries vs. ground-truth counts of 27 and 10 respectively.

🚫
Missing Intervals

Bins 1 (1–2 k), 2 (2–3 k), and 9 (9–10 k) have zero profiling coverage. Bin 1 and 2 are also empty in ground truth, but Bin 9 has 10 ground-truth queries with no profiling match.

📝
Stage Limitation

Profiling only identifies what costs templates can produce — it does not yet control how many queries land in each bin. That balancing act is the responsibility of the refinement stage.

Template Contribution Analysis
Discarded Templates

Three templates generated costs that fell outside the valid normalized range [0, 10 000]. Their queries were excluded from the profiling histogram.

Template 3 Below Minimum
⬇ Cost Underflow

Generated execution costs fell below the minimum cost observed in the original workload. After normalization, queries mapped below Bin 0, placing them outside the [0, 10 000] valid range and making them incomparable to the ground-truth histogram.

Template 10 Above Maximum
⬆ Cost Overflow

Generated costs exceeded the maximum cost in the original workload. Post-normalization, these values surpassed 10 000, placing them beyond the histogram ceiling. Including them would distort the distribution comparison.

Template 29 Above Maximum
⬆ Cost Overflow

Same overflow condition as Template 10 — normalized costs exceeded 10 000. This template also produced queries beyond the valid ceiling and was excluded from the profiling histogram to maintain distribution integrity.

Key Findings
Profiling Stage Summary
  • 01Bins 0 and 5 are overrepresented in profiling, suggesting these cost ranges are disproportionately easy for the current template set to cover.
  • 02Bin 9 (9 000–10 000) exists in the ground truth with 10 queries but is completely absent in profiling — a critical gap that the refinement stage must address.
  • 03Three templates (3, 10, 29) produced out-of-range costs and were discarded, reducing the effective template pool and contributing to some of the uncovered bins.
  • 04The profiling distribution covers 7 out of 10 bins, but with uneven density — the shape does not yet resemble the ground-truth distribution.
  • 05Bins 3 and 8 are significantly underrepresented, meaning templates capable of producing mid-to-high cost queries need to be identified or refined.
  • 06Despite mismatches, the profiling stage successfully establishes a baseline coverage map — confirming which cost intervals the current template pool can reach.
Next Stage
Template Refinement

What Refinement Will Do

  • 1Close coverage gaps — Identify or modify templates to produce queries in Bin 9 and other missing intervals, ensuring every ground-truth bin has at least some profiling representation.
  • 2Rebalance overrepresented bins — Reduce the proportion of queries falling in Bins 0 and 5 by adjusting template parameters or sampling weights.
  • 3Address discarded templates — Investigate Templates 3, 10, and 29 to understand whether parameter tuning can bring their costs within the valid normalization range.
  • 4Minimize histogram divergence — Iteratively adjust the generated workload composition until its distribution closely approximates the ground-truth histogram, using a distance metric (e.g., KL divergence or histogram intersection).
